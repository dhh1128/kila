## Word order
Word order is SVO (subject-verb-object). This is approximately the same approach to ordering that's used by English, Mandarin, Russian, Spanish, and Hindi. It's the most common word order in the world's languages. However, kila is far more regular in this choice, whereas most other languages are not. See the [sample dialog from Jane Austen](samples/random/pride-and-prejudice.md) for some examples of English variation and kila consistency.

Adjectives and adverbs (or phrases that play an adjectival or adverbial role) always come after the noun or verb that they modify, and they are appended in order from most categorical/intrinsic to least categorical/intrinsic (a subjective judgment of the speaker). More categorical/intrinsic properties bind closest to what they modify; more incidental/variable properties bind farther away.

    English: beautiful big dogs
    Kila: *vxdag gran bel* (literally, 'dogs big beautiful' — 'big' is more categorical, 'beautiful' more subjective)

Adjectives and adverbs are the same words in kila, not separate derivations. Unlike English, there is no "weak" (adjective) vs. "weakly" (adverb). Position determines function, and the distinction is not considered significant. Words can modify nouns (as adjectives), verbs (as adverbs), or other adjectives (as adverbs), always following what they modify:

* Adjective modifying noun: *dag gran* 'big dog'
* Adverb modifying verb: *run velis* 'run quickly'
* Adverb modifying adjective: *laden hevyli* 'heavily laden'

Past participles are equivalent to infinitives (both use the *-n* form) and can be used as adjectives:

* *liv skribdn* 'a written book' (literally, 'book written')
* *ant chozn* 'the chosen person'

Kila uses prepositions, not postpositions.

## Negation

Negation comes before what's negated. The position of *no* determines what is being negated (scope):

* *i no hav nixn* 'I don't have a friend' — negates the verb (the having is negated)
* *i hav no nixn* 'I have no friend' — negates the noun (what exists is not-a-friend)
* *id felys bi no nor* 'My cat is non-black' — negates the adjective

## Passive voice

Passive voice is formed by preceding the verb with *dx*. The logical subject can be omitted or optionally included with a prepositional phrase:

* *liv d skribdy* 'the book was written'
* *i d nasdy* 'I was born'

The passive construction is also used for impersonal constructions that have no logical subject, instead of using an impersonal "it" pronoun:

* *d reingo* 'it's raining' (literally, 'rains')
* *d sedy* 'it was said'

## Questions

Yes/no questions are formed by adding the particle *yz* at the beginning of the sentence. Word order does not change.

* Statement: *w hav nixn* 'you have a friend'
* Question: *yz w hav nixn?* 'do you have a friend?'

Wh-questions begin with question words like *hu* 'who', *uat* 'what', *huar* 'where', etc. They do not use *yz*:

* *hu sedy iat?* 'who said that?'
* *uat w vyzdy?* 'what did you see?'

## Relative clauses

Relative clauses are introduced by *kx*, which functions like English "that" or "which":

* *dag kx i vyzdy* 'the dog that I saw'
* *ant kx sedy iat* 'the person who said that'

## Comparatives

Comparatives are formed with the prefix *plu-* on the adjective. The word *kx* is used for 'than':

* *i bi plubyg kx e* 'I am bigger than her'
* *dag bi pluvelis kx felys* 'the dog is faster than the cat'

Superlatives use the prefix *pli-* ('most'):

* *e bi plibyg* 'she is the biggest'
* *ia bi plijust* 'this is the most just'

Equatives use *so...kwa* ('as...as'):

* *i bi so byg kwa e* 'I am as big as her'
* *dag bi so velis kwa felys* 'the dog is as fast as the cat'

## Demonstratives

Demonstratives come before the noun they modify:

* *ki dag* 'this dog'
* *ia ant* 'that person'
* *vxki dag* 'these dogs'
* *vxia ant* 'those people'

The pronouns *kit* 'this', *iat* 'that', *vxkit* 'these', *vxiat* 'those' can stand alone without a noun.

## Articles

The definite article is *zx* 'the'. Articles are rarely used in kila; when *zx* is used, it acts like an intensifier, emphasizing definiteness or uniqueness:

* *zx dag* 'THE dog' (the specific, definite dog we're talking about)

## Subordinate clauses

Temporal conjunctions: *uen* 'when', *dr* 'while/during', *pre* 'before', *post* 'after', *aun* 'until'

Causal: *pask* 'because/since', *donk* 'so/so that/therefore'

Conditional: *yf* 'if', *sof* 'unless/except'

## Indirect objects

Indirect objects follow the direct object and are preceded by *dx*:

* *i gyvdy buk dx meri* 'I gave a book to Mary' (literally: 'I gave book to Mary')

## Prepositional phrases

Prepositional phrases attach following the same binding principles as adjectives and adverbs. They are positioned based on how categorical/intrinsic vs. incidental/variable their meaning is to the modified element.

## Agreement
In kila, there is no such thing as grammatical gender or number, and there are no agreement rules. Adjectives do not get inflected to agree with nouns or pronouns.

Plural marking is used minimally — only enough to make meaning clear. In English, we say "many hands make light work," but in kila it would be "many hand..." because 'many' already conveys plurality. Redundant plural marking is avoided.
